# Dreyer's English: A Style Reference

_An original distillation of principles from Benjamin Dreyer's approach to
English usage and style. This is not excerpted text — it is a reference guide
inspired by Dreyer's positions on grammar, punctuation, and usage._

## I. The First Step: Cut the Clutter

Before revising for anything else, delete words that add no meaning. These
words appear reflexively in drafts. Removing them almost never changes the
meaning of a sentence, but it almost always makes the sentence better.

### The Go-To Deletions

Try removing these words from any sentence. If the sentence survives — and it
usually does — leave them out:

- **very**
- **rather**
- **really**
- **quite**
- **just**
- **in fact**
- **actually**
- **basically**
- **essentially**
- **totally**
- **completely**
- **absolutely**
- **literally** (when used for emphasis rather than literal truth)
- **definitely**
- **certainly**
- **obviously**
- **clearly**
- **of course**
- **indeed**
- **surely**
- **needless to say** (then don't say it)

This is not a banned-words list. Each of these words has legitimate uses.
"Very" before "first" in "the very first time" changes the meaning. "Just"
in "just three days" does real work. The test: does removing the word change
what you mean? If not, cut it.

### Throat-Clearing Phrases

These phrases delay the sentence's point:

| Cut this                           | Keep this (if anything) |
| ---------------------------------- | ----------------------- |
| It should be noted that            | (nothing — just state it) |
| It is interesting to note that     | (nothing — just state it) |
| It goes without saying that        | (nothing — just state it) |
| The thing is                       | (nothing — just state it) |
| What I'm trying to say is         | (nothing — just say it) |
| At the end of the day              | Ultimately              |
| When all is said and done          | (cut entirely)          |
| It is what it is                   | (cut entirely)          |
| In terms of                        | (rewrite the sentence)  |

### "That Said" and Its Cousins

Transitional phrases like "that said," "having said that," "be that as it
may," and "with that being said" are usually filler. If the contrast is
real, "but" or "however" does the job. If the contrast is imagined, the
transitional phrase is masking the absence of an actual turn in the argument.

## II. Rules That Aren't Rules

Several "rules" of English grammar are not rules at all. They are
superstitions inherited from well-meaning but misguided style advice.
A modern style guide should acknowledge what is real and what is invented.

### Split Infinitives Are Fine

"To boldly go" is correct English. It has always been correct English. The
prohibition against splitting infinitives was imported from Latin grammar,
where infinitives are single words and cannot be split. English infinitives
are two words, and inserting an adverb between them is natural and often
produces the clearest sentence.

Split when clarity demands it:

- "She decided to gradually increase the dose" is clearer than "She decided
  gradually to increase the dose" or "She decided to increase gradually the
  dose."

Do not split when the result is awkward:

- "He wanted to, if at all possible, avoid the meeting" — restructure this.

### Ending Sentences with Prepositions Is Fine

"What are you looking at?" is correct. "At what are you looking?" is
stilted. The prohibition against terminal prepositions is another Latin
import that does not apply to English. End with a preposition when it
sounds natural. Restructure only when the preposition dangles meaninglessly:

- Fine: "That's the project I was working on."
- Awkward: "That's the project on which I was working." (restructured
  without need)
- Bad: "Where is it at?" (the "at" adds nothing)

### Starting Sentences with "And" or "But" Is Fine

Conjunctions at the start of a sentence are a legitimate stylistic choice.
They create emphasis and momentum. "But" at the start of a sentence signals
a turn more sharply than "however" buried mid-sentence.

Use them deliberately, not as a tic. If every other sentence starts with
"And" or "But," the device loses its force.

### "They" as a Singular Pronoun Is Fine

Singular "they" has been used in English since at least the 14th century.
It is the natural choice when the gender of the antecedent is unknown,
unspecified, or nonbinary:

- "Everyone should bring their own laptop."
- "If a customer calls, tell them we'll call back."
- "Alex said they would be late."

The alternatives — "he or she," "s/he," or defaulting to "he" — are
clumsy, exclusionary, or both.

### "Data" Can Be Singular

"Data" is technically a Latin plural (singular: "datum"), but in modern
English it functions as a mass noun, like "information" or "evidence."
Both "the data is clear" and "the data are clear" are acceptable. Be
consistent within a document.

## III. Punctuation

### The Serial Comma

Use it. Always. The serial comma (also called the Oxford comma) appears
before the conjunction in a list of three or more items:

- "Red, white, and blue" — clear
- "Red, white and blue" — potentially ambiguous

Without the serial comma, the last two items can appear to be a unit:
"I admire my parents, Gandhi and Mother Teresa" suggests your parents
are Gandhi and Mother Teresa. The serial comma prevents this: "I admire
my parents, Gandhi, and Mother Teresa."

There are rare cases where the serial comma creates ambiguity rather than
resolving it. In those cases, rewrite the sentence rather than dropping
the comma.

### The Semicolon

The semicolon joins two independent clauses that are closely related in
thought. It signals: "these two sentences are so connected that a period
would create too much separation."

**Correct:**

> The project launched on time; the team celebrated with pizza.

**Incorrect:**

> The project launched on time; which surprised everyone.

(The second clause is dependent — use a comma, not a semicolon.)

The semicolon also separates items in a complex list where items contain
internal commas:

> The committee included Dr. Smith, a biologist; Ms. Patel, an engineer;
> and Mr. Liu, an economist.

Do not overuse semicolons. If you use more than one per paragraph
regularly, you are probably combining sentences that deserve to stand
alone.

### The Em Dash

The em dash (—) sets off a parenthetical with more emphasis than commas
and less formality than parentheses. Use it for:

- **Interruptions**: "The deadline — which no one thought was realistic
  — came and went."
- **Amplification**: "She had one goal — promotion."
- **Lists after a colon substitute**: "Three factors drove the decision —
  cost, speed, and team morale."

Limit yourself to one pair of em dashes per sentence. Two pairs create
a nest that the reader cannot parse.

Do not use an em dash where a comma, colon, or period would serve better.
The em dash is a spice, not a staple. Overuse signals that the writer
is avoiding the discipline of choosing the right punctuation.

### The En Dash

The en dash (–) is not a short em dash. It has specific uses:

- **Ranges**: "pages 12–34," "2020–2025," "Monday–Friday"
- **Compound modifiers where one element is multi-word**: "New York–based
  company," "post–World War II era"

Do not use a hyphen where an en dash belongs. Do not use an em dash where
an en dash belongs. They are three distinct marks with distinct purposes.

### Hyphens in Compound Modifiers

Hyphenate compound modifiers before a noun:

- "well-known author" but "the author is well known"
- "high-quality code" but "the code is high quality"
- "long-term plan" but "the plan is long term"

Do not hyphenate when the first word is an adverb ending in "-ly":

- "newly created file" (no hyphen)
- "highly rated product" (no hyphen)

### The Apostrophe

The apostrophe marks possession and contraction. It does not mark plurals.

- **Decades**: "the 1990s" not "the 1990's"
- **Acronyms**: "URLs" not "URL's"
- **Letters used as words**: "mind your p's and q's" (exception — the
  apostrophe prevents misreading)

For possessives of singular nouns ending in "s": add "'s" in most cases.
"The boss's office," "James's book," "the witness's testimony." Exceptions
exist for classical and biblical names by tradition: "Jesus' teachings,"
"Euripides' plays."

## IV. Grammar

### Subject-Verb Agreement

The verb agrees with the subject, not with the nearest noun:

- "The box of chocolates **is** on the table" (not "are" — the subject is
  "box," not "chocolates")
- "The data from all three studies **suggest** a trend" (if treating "data"
  as plural) or "The data **suggests** a trend" (if treating it as a mass
  noun)

Collective nouns take singular or plural verbs depending on whether you
mean the group as a unit or the individuals within it. Be consistent:

- "The team is meeting at noon" (the team as a unit)
- "The team are divided on the approach" (the individuals disagree)

American English generally treats collective nouns as singular. British
English is more flexible.

### Pronoun Case

Use the correct case:

- **Subject**: "She and I went to the meeting" (not "Her and me went")
- **Object**: "The task was assigned to her and me" (not "to she and I")
- **Test**: Remove the other person. "I went to the meeting" (correct).
  "Me went to the meeting" (wrong). "The task was assigned to me" (correct).
  "The task was assigned to I" (wrong).

"Between you and me" is always correct. "Between you and I" is always wrong.
The preposition "between" takes the objective case.

### Who vs. Whom

"Who" is the subject form. "Whom" is the object form.

- "Who wrote this?" ("who" is the subject of "wrote")
- "To whom should I send it?" ("whom" is the object of "to")
- **Test**: Substitute "he" or "him." If "him" works, use "whom." If "he"
  works, use "who." "He wrote this" → "who." "Send it to him" → "whom."

In informal writing, "who" in place of "whom" is widely accepted. "Who
should I send it to?" reads naturally. But in formal or professional
writing, correct usage matters.

### Dangling Modifiers

A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the
grammatical subject:

- **Wrong**: "Walking into the room, the painting caught my eye."
  (The painting was not walking.)
- **Right**: "Walking into the room, I noticed the painting."

This rule applies to all introductory modifiers, not just participial
phrases:

- **Wrong**: "As a long-time customer, your loyalty program interests me."
  (The loyalty program is not a customer.)
- **Right**: "As a long-time customer, I find your loyalty program
  interesting."

## V. Words and Expressions

### Commonly Confused Pairs

| Correct Usage | Common Error |
| --- | --- |
| **affect** (verb: to influence) | Confused with "effect" (noun: result) |
| **effect** (verb: to bring about) | "Effect change" not "affect change" |
| **complement** (to complete) | Confused with "compliment" (to praise) |
| **principal** (main; head of school) | Confused with "principle" (rule, belief) |
| **discreet** (tactful, careful) | Confused with "discrete" (separate, distinct) |
| **stationary** (not moving) | Confused with "stationery" (paper, writing materials) |
| **farther** (physical distance) | Confused with "further" (abstract degree) |
| **fewer** (countable items) | Confused with "less" (uncountable quantity) |
| **comprise** (the whole comprises its parts) | "Composed of" not "comprised of" |
| **ensure** (make certain) | Confused with "insure" (insurance) and "assure" (tell someone) |
| **imply** (speaker implies) | Confused with "infer" (listener infers) |
| **lie** (to recline; lay, lain) | Confused with "lay" (to place; laid, laid) |
| **flout** (to defy openly) | Confused with "flaunt" (to show off) |
| **reticent** (reluctant to speak) | Often misused to mean "reluctant" generally |
| **bemused** (confused, bewildered) | Often misused to mean "amused" |
| **peruse** (to read carefully) | Often misused to mean "to skim" |
| **penultimate** (second to last) | Often misused to mean "ultimate" or "best" |
| **fortuitous** (happening by chance) | Often misused to mean "fortunate" |

### Expressions to Reconsider

| Instead of | Consider |
| --- | --- |
| "irregardless" | "regardless" (irregardless is nonstandard) |
| "could of" | "could have" (the "of" is a mishearing) |
| "for all intensive purposes" | "for all intents and purposes" |
| "one in the same" | "one and the same" |
| "case in point" followed by a colon | "case in point" is not followed by a colon |
| "beg the question" (to raise) | Traditionally means to assume the conclusion; "raise the question" is clearer for the common meaning |
| "hone in" | "home in" (a homing pigeon homes in, not hones in) |
| "snuck" | "sneaked" is the traditional past tense, though "snuck" is now widely accepted in informal writing |
| "on accident" | "by accident" (standard) |
| "first-come, first-serve" | "first-come, first-served" (the people are served, not serving) |

### Words That Don't Mean What You Think

- **Nonplussed**: Means bewildered or at a loss — not unfazed or indifferent.
  The informal American usage (meaning unfazed) is almost the opposite of
  the traditional meaning. Avoid the word entirely to prevent confusion.

- **Enormity**: Traditionally means extreme evil or moral outrageousness,
  not enormous size. "The enormity of the stadium" is a misuse. Use
  "enormousness" or "immensity" for size.

- **Fulsome**: Means excessively flattering or insincere — not full or
  abundant. "Fulsome praise" is not a compliment; it means the praise is
  sycophantic.

- **Noisome**: Means foul-smelling or noxious — not noisy. Nothing to do
  with noise.

- **Infamous**: Means famous for something bad — not merely famous. An
  infamous person has a reputation for wrongdoing.

## VI. The Tricky Stuff

### Numbers

- Spell out one through ten (or one hundred, depending on formality).
  Use numerals for larger numbers.
- Never start a sentence with a numeral. Spell it out or restructure:
  "Forty percent of respondents..." or "Of the respondents, 40%..."
- Be consistent within a passage. If one number in a series requires
  numerals, use numerals for all: "We tested 8, 12, and 15 configurations"
  (not "eight, 12, and 15").
- Use numerals for measurements, ages, dates, times, and addresses.

### Italics vs. Quotation Marks

- **Italics** for titles of long works: books, films, albums, newspapers,
  magazines, TV series, plays
- **Quotation marks** for titles of short works: articles, chapters, songs,
  poems, episodes, short stories
- **Italics** for foreign words and phrases not yet absorbed into English
- **Italics** for words used as words: "The word *integrity* appears seven
  times in the mission statement"

### One Space After a Period

One space. Not two. The two-space convention comes from typewriters, where
monospaced fonts needed extra space for readability. Modern proportional
fonts do not. Two spaces after a period is not wrong in the way a
misspelling is wrong, but it marks the writer as either old-fashioned or
inattentive to contemporary practice.

### Spell Out Abbreviations on First Use

On first reference, spell out the full term followed by the abbreviation
in parentheses: "customer relationship management (CRM)." After that, the
abbreviation alone is fine. Exception: universally known abbreviations
(NASA, FBI, CEO, URL) do not need spelling out for a general audience,
though they may for a specialized audience unfamiliar with the term.

### Consistency Above All

When the style guides disagree — and they will — pick one approach and
apply it consistently throughout the document. Inconsistency is more
distracting than any individual style choice. A document that uses "email"
in one paragraph and "e-mail" in the next signals carelessness regardless
of which spelling is "correct."
