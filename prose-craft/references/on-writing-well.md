# On Writing Well: Genre Conventions

_An original distillation of genre-specific writing conventions inspired
by William Zinsser's approach to nonfiction. This is not excerpted text —
it is a reference guide for adopting the voice and conventions of specific
nonfiction genres._

## I. The Universal Principles

These principles apply across all nonfiction genres. They are the
foundation on which genre-specific conventions build.

### Simplicity

Clear thinking produces clear writing. Unclear writing almost always
reflects unclear thinking. When a sentence is hard to parse, the problem
is rarely vocabulary or grammar — it is that the writer has not decided
what they mean.

The first revision pass should always ask: can this be said more simply?
Not more briefly — more simply. A complex idea can be expressed simply.
A simple idea expressed in complex language is pretension.

### Clutter

Every profession and institution generates its own clutter: words and
phrases that sound important but add nothing. Cutting clutter is not
about making sentences shorter — it is about making every word earn its
place.

Common clutter by domain:

| Domain | Clutter | Clean |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Business | "at this point in time" | "now" |
| Business | "in the event that" | "if" |
| Business | "on a going-forward basis" | "from now on" |
| Academic | "it is important to note that" | (just state it) |
| Academic | "the question of whether" | "whether" |
| Academic | "a not insignificant number" | "many" or a specific number |
| Technology | "provides the ability to" | "can" |
| Technology | "in order to facilitate" | "to help" |
| Technology | "leveraging our platform" | "using our platform" |
| Government | "at the present time" | "now" |
| Government | "for the purpose of" | "to" |
| Government | "with regard to" | "about" |
| Marketing | "best-in-class solution" | (name what it actually does) |
| Marketing | "empower your team" | (name what they can do) |
| Marketing | "drive meaningful results" | (name the results) |

### The Audience of One

Write for yourself first. If something amuses you, interests you, or
moves you, there is a good chance it will have the same effect on a
reader. Writing that tries to please everyone pleases no one — it
becomes cautious, hedged, and lifeless.

This does not mean ignoring the reader. It means finding the intersection
between what interests you and what the reader needs. The energy of
genuine engagement is what distinguishes writing that is read from
writing that is skimmed.

## II. Genre Conventions

Each nonfiction genre has conventions that readers expect. These are not
arbitrary rules — they evolved because they serve the genre's purpose.
Violating them confuses readers. Honoring them creates trust.

### Technology and Science Writing

**Purpose:** Make complex ideas accessible without dumbing them down.

**Conventions:**

- Lead with the concept, not the history. Readers want to understand what
  something is before they learn how it came to be.
- Use concrete examples early. An abstract principle followed by an example
  is easier to grasp than an abstract principle alone, but an example
  followed by the principle it illustrates is even better.
- Analogies are powerful but dangerous. A good analogy illuminates the
  unfamiliar through the familiar. A bad analogy introduces a second
  thing the reader must understand. Test every analogy: does the reader
  already know the thing you are comparing to?
- Define terms when they first appear, but naturally — not with "which is
  defined as" or parenthetical definitions. Work the definition into the
  sentence: "The cache — a fast-access copy of frequently used data —
  reduces response times."
- Avoid the false choice between precision and readability. Precise writing
  is not jargon-heavy writing. The most precise sentence is often the
  simplest one.
- Numbers need context. "The server handles 50,000 requests per second"
  means nothing without comparison. "The server handles 50,000 requests
  per second — ten times the previous system" gives the reader a frame.

**Voice:** Confident, curious, explanatory. The writer is a knowledgeable
guide, not a lecturer. First person ("I," "we") is appropriate. Enthusiasm
is appropriate. Jargon is not.

### Business and Strategy Writing

**Purpose:** Inform decisions. Every sentence should move the reader
toward understanding a situation, evaluating options, or taking action.

**Conventions:**

- State the recommendation or conclusion first, then support it. Business
  readers scan before they read. If the key insight is on page three, most
  readers will never reach it.
- Quantify claims. "Revenue increased" is weak. "Revenue increased 23% in
  Q3" is useful. "Revenue increased 23% in Q3, driven by the enterprise
  segment" is actionable.
- Distinguish facts from opinions explicitly. "Market share grew 5%"
  (fact). "We believe this trend will continue" (opinion). Mixing them
  without labels erodes trust.
- Avoid corporate euphemism. "Right-sizing" means layoffs. "Synergies"
  means cost cuts. "Learnings" means mistakes. Call things what they are.
- Structure for scanning: clear headings, bullet points for parallel
  items, bold for key terms. A business document should be useful at
  three levels of reading: scan (headings only), skim (first sentence
  of each paragraph), and read (full text).

**Voice:** Direct, evidence-based, confident but not arrogant. Hedging
("perhaps," "it could be argued") signals lack of conviction. State
the position, support it, acknowledge risks.

### Educational and Tutorial Writing

**Purpose:** Transfer a skill or understanding. The reader should be able
to do something after reading that they could not do before.

**Conventions:**

- Sequence matters more than in any other genre. Each concept must build
  on the previous one. If the reader needs to understand A before B, and
  B before C, the order is non-negotiable.
- Show, then explain. A code example, a screenshot, a worked problem —
  show the thing first, then explain what is happening and why. The
  concrete instance makes the abstract explanation stick.
- Anticipate errors. When a step can go wrong, say what will happen and
  how to fix it. This is not pessimism — it is respect for the reader's
  experience.
- Use the second person ("you"). The reader is doing the work. "You will
  create a new file" is more direct than "A new file should be created"
  or "The developer creates a new file."
- Keep each step atomic. One action per step. If a step says "Create the
  file and configure the settings and restart the service," it is three
  steps pretending to be one.

**Voice:** Patient, precise, encouraging without being condescending.
Avoid "simply" and "just" — these words trivialize difficulty and
frustrate readers who are struggling.

### Persuasive and Opinion Writing

**Purpose:** Move the reader to accept a position or take an action.

**Conventions:**

- State the thesis early and explicitly. The reader should know what you
  are arguing for within the first paragraph. Building to a "reveal"
  loses readers who want to evaluate the argument as they read.
- Acknowledge the strongest counterargument. Ignoring objections makes
  the writer look uninformed or evasive. Addressing them builds
  credibility: "The obvious objection is X. Here's why it doesn't hold."
- Use evidence, not adjectives. "This approach is better" is assertion.
  "This approach reduced errors by 40% in our testing" is evidence.
  Let the evidence do the persuading.
- Distinguish between logical argument and emotional appeal. Both are
  legitimate, but mixing them without awareness produces muddled prose.
  A data-driven argument should not suddenly pivot to an emotional plea.
- End with a call to action or a clear implication. What should the
  reader do, think, or believe differently after reading this?

**Voice:** Confident, fair-minded, substantive. The writer has a position
but respects the reader's ability to evaluate it. Passionate is fine.
Polemical is risky. Sanctimonious is fatal.

### Narrative and Story-Driven Writing

**Purpose:** Engage the reader through story, creating emotional
connection and memorable understanding.

**Conventions:**

- Start with a scene, not a summary. "On the morning of March 15, the
  server room was 40 degrees too warm" is a scene. "Server reliability
  has been a persistent challenge" is a summary. Scenes pull readers in.
- Use specific, sensory details. Not "the office was busy" but "three
  monitors showed red alerts, the on-call phone buzzed every thirty
  seconds, and someone had left a half-eaten sandwich on the keyboard."
- People drive stories. Name the people involved. Give them actions,
  decisions, consequences. An abstract process becomes a story when a
  person is at the center of it.
- Maintain chronological order unless there is a compelling reason to
  break it. Flashbacks and flash-forwards are literary devices that
  require skill to execute. When in doubt, tell it in order.
- The ending must deliver on the promise of the opening. If the opening
  poses a question, the ending answers it. If the opening presents a
  problem, the ending resolves it (or explains why it remains unresolved).

**Voice:** Vivid, specific, human. The writer is a storyteller. First
person is natural when the writer witnessed the events. Third person
works when reporting others' experiences.

### Internal Documentation

**Purpose:** Enable a colleague to understand, maintain, or extend
something without asking the original author.

**Conventions:**

- Write for the reader who arrives at 2 AM during an incident. They need
  to understand the system quickly, find the relevant section, and act
  on it. This means: clear headings, direct answers, no preamble.
- Explain *why*, not just *what*. "The timeout is set to 30 seconds"
  is a fact. "The timeout is set to 30 seconds because the downstream
  service occasionally takes 20 seconds under load" is documentation.
  The *why* is what prevents the next person from changing it to 5 seconds.
- Keep it current or mark it stale. Out-of-date documentation is worse
  than no documentation — it actively misleads. If you cannot commit to
  maintaining a document, add a "last verified" date so readers can
  calibrate trust.
- Separate reference material from tutorials. A reference document
  answers "what is the parameter for X?" A tutorial answers "how do I
  set up X from scratch?" These serve different needs and should be
  different documents.

**Voice:** Clear, direct, no personality required. The writing should
be invisible — the reader should be thinking about the system, not
about the document.

## III. Finding the Right Register

Register is the level of formality in your writing. It ranges from
casual (conversational, first person, contractions) to formal (impersonal,
third person, no contractions). The right register depends on the audience
and context.

### Register Spectrum

| Level | Features | Appropriate For |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Casual | Contractions, first person, slang, sentence fragments | Blog posts, team chat, internal notes |
| Conversational | Contractions, first person, natural rhythm, no slang | Articles, tutorials, most web content |
| Professional | Selective contractions, first or third person, measured tone | Reports, proposals, business communication |
| Formal | No contractions, third person, precise vocabulary | Academic papers, legal documents, regulatory filings |

### Calibrating Register

- Match the register to the publication, not the topic. A blog post about
  quantum computing should be conversational. An academic paper about a
  simple topic should be formal.
- Stay consistent. A document that shifts between registers — formal in
  the introduction, casual in the body — feels uncontrolled.
- When uncertain, choose one level more casual than your instinct suggests.
  Most writers err toward excessive formality, which creates distance
  between writer and reader.

## IV. The Writer's Voice

Voice is the quality that makes one writer's prose sound different from
another's, even when they are writing about the same topic in the same
genre. It is not something you adopt — it is something you discover by
writing honestly and cutting everything that is not you.

### Voice Is Not Style

Style is the set of rules you follow (active voice, short sentences,
serial comma). Voice is the personality that emerges when you apply those
rules. Two writers can follow the same style guide and produce prose
that sounds entirely different.

### Developing Voice

- Write in the first person when possible. "I" forces you to take
  ownership of your claims and opinions. "It is believed that" hides
  behind an anonymous collective.
- Read your writing aloud. If it doesn't sound like something you would
  say, it doesn't sound like you. Rewrite until it does.
- Cut every word that is there to impress rather than to communicate.
  Voice emerges when pretension is removed.
- Trust your enthusiasms. If a topic excites you, that energy belongs
  in the writing. If a topic bores you, either find the angle that
  interests you or acknowledge that someone else should write it.

### Voice and Community Convention

Voice is individual, but it exists within community conventions. A
technology blog has a different voice convention than an academic journal.
A startup's documentation has a different voice convention than a
government agency's.

Understanding the community convention is essential. It tells you:

- What level of informality is welcome
- Whether humor is appropriate
- Whether first person is expected or avoided
- Whether jargon is assumed or must be defined
- What kind of evidence the community values

Adopt the community's conventions for structure, formality, and evidence.
Let your individual voice come through in word choice, rhythm, and the
specific details you notice.
