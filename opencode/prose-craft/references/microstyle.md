# Microstyle: Short-Form Writing Craft

_An original distillation of techniques for writing headlines, taglines,
product names, and other short-form text. Inspired by Christopher Johnson's
approach to the craft of brief, impactful writing. This is not excerpted
text — it is a reference guide for short-form composition._

## I. Why Short-Form Is Different

Long-form writing builds meaning through context, evidence, and
argument. Short-form writing must create meaning in a few words —
sometimes a single phrase. The constraints are different, and the
techniques are different.

In short-form writing:

- **Every word carries maximum weight.** In a 500-word article, one weak
  word is noise. In a five-word headline, one weak word is 20% of the
  message.
- **Sound matters.** Readers hear headlines and taglines in their heads.
  Rhythm, alliteration, and vowel sounds affect how the text lands.
- **Ambiguity can be a tool.** Long-form writing resolves ambiguity.
  Short-form writing sometimes exploits it — a headline with two
  meanings can stop a reader in their tracks.
- **Convention signals genre.** A headline that sounds like a headline
  gets read as a headline. A tagline that sounds like a sentence gets
  read as a sentence. Matching the expected form tells the reader how
  to process the text.

## II. Meaning Techniques

### Compression

Say more with fewer words by choosing words that carry multiple meanings
or implications.

**Techniques:**

- **Loaded words**: Words that carry emotional or cultural weight beyond
  their dictionary meaning. "Home" versus "house." "Craft" versus "make."
  "Discover" versus "find."
- **Implied narratives**: A phrase that suggests a story without telling
  it. "Before your first cup of coffee" implies morning routine,
  simplicity, speed — in six words.
- **Category tension**: Combining words from different domains. "Kitchen
  confidence" puts a cooking context next to an emotional state, creating
  a meaning that neither word carries alone.

### Specificity

Specific details are more memorable and credible than general claims.

| General | Specific |
| --- | --- |
| Fast delivery | Delivered by Tuesday |
| Expert support | Talk to an engineer |
| Affordable | Starting at $9/month |
| Better performance | 3x faster queries |
| Used by many | Trusted by 50,000 teams |

Specificity works because it gives the reader something concrete to
evaluate. "Fast delivery" is a claim. "Delivered by Tuesday" is a
promise.

### Surprise

The most memorable short-form text violates an expectation. The reader
predicts where the sentence is going, and the ending goes somewhere else.

**Techniques:**

- **Reversal**: Set up an expected pattern, then reverse it. "Think
  different." (Expected: "Think differently." The adjective instead of
  the adverb creates surprise.)
- **Reframing**: Take a familiar concept and present it from an
  unexpected angle. "The best camera is the one you have with you."
  (Reframes a technology question as a proximity question.)
- **Juxtaposition**: Place two unexpected things next to each other.
  The tension between them creates meaning. "Serious play." "Beautiful
  data." "Quiet power."

### Evocation Over Description

Short-form text works better when it evokes a feeling or image rather
than describing a feature.

| Descriptive | Evocative |
| --- | --- |
| Noise-canceling headphones | Silence, delivered |
| High-resolution display | Every pixel, visible |
| Fast file transfer | Your files, already there |
| Secure encryption | Your secrets, kept |

Evocative language lets the reader fill in the details with their own
experience. This creates a stronger connection than listing specifications.

## III. Sound Techniques

Short-form text is heard, even when read silently. The sound of a
phrase affects how memorable, credible, and pleasurable it is.

### Alliteration

Repeating the initial consonant sound creates rhythm and memorability.

- "PayPal" (P-P)
- "Best Buy" (B-B)
- "Coca-Cola" (C-C, hard K sound)
- "Bed Bath & Beyond" (B-B-B)

Use alliteration when you want the phrase to stick. Avoid it when the
repetition sounds forced or cartoonish — two alliterative words is
elegant, four is Dr. Seuss.

### Assonance

Repeating vowel sounds within words creates internal rhyme without
the sing-song quality of end rhyme.

- "YouTube" (long OO repeated)
- "Take a break" (long A repeated)
- "Bold gold" (long O repeated)

Assonance is subtler than alliteration and works well in taglines
where overt sound play would feel too clever.

### Rhythm and Meter

Short phrases have a natural rhythm. Matching that rhythm to the
message strengthens the impact.

- **Iambic (da-DUM)**: Natural, conversational. "Just DO it."
  "Think BIG."
- **Trochaic (DUM-da)**: Assertive, commanding. "GOT milk?"
  "THINK different."
- **Three beats**: Satisfying, complete. "Snap, Crackle, Pop."
  "Stop, Drop, Roll." "Reduce, Reuse, Recycle."

Read your headline or tagline aloud. Tap the stressed syllables. Does
the rhythm feel natural? Does it reinforce the meaning? A tagline about
speed should feel fast. A tagline about luxury should feel unhurried.

### Rhyme (Used Sparingly)

Full rhyme in short-form text risks sounding juvenile, but near-rhyme
and internal rhyme can be effective:

- **Near-rhyme**: "Don't dream it. Drive it." (dream/drive share the
  DR onset but don't rhyme)
- **Internal rhyme**: "The best rest" (hidden rhyme within the phrase)

If the rhyme sounds like an advertising jingle from the 1960s, cut it.
Modern short-form text prefers subtler sound techniques.

## IV. Structure Techniques

### Parallelism

Parallel structure in short-form text creates balance and memorability.

- "Easy to learn. Hard to master." (adjective + infinitive, repeated)
- "More space. Less clutter." (comparative + noun, repeated)
- "Read anywhere. Write everywhere." (verb + anywhere/everywhere)

The parallel structure tells the reader that the two halves are related
and should be compared. Breaking the parallelism in the second half
creates surprise (see Reversal under Surprise).

### Fragments

Complete sentences are optional in short-form text. Fragments can be
more powerful because they strip away syntactic scaffolding and leave
only the essential meaning.

- "Impossible is nothing." (Fragment used as declaration)
- "Because you're worth it." (Dependent clause standing alone)
- "The ultimate driving machine." (Noun phrase as identity statement)

Use fragments when the meaning is clear without a full sentence. Use
complete sentences when the fragment would be ambiguous.

### The Power of Three

Three items in a list feel complete and balanced. Two feel incomplete.
Four feel like a list. Three feel like a statement.

- "Life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness."
- "Blood, sweat, and tears."
- "Location, location, location."

When you have more than three features or benefits to communicate, choose
the three that matter most. The constraint forces prioritization, and
prioritization creates clarity.

### Brevity Hierarchy

When cutting words, remove in this order:

1. **Articles** (a, an, the) — often unnecessary in headlines
2. **Auxiliary verbs** (is, are, was, were, has, have) — headlines use
   present tense without auxiliaries
3. **Prepositions** — sometimes replaceable with juxtaposition
4. **Adjectives** — keep only the one that does the most work
5. **Nouns and verbs** — these are load-bearing; cut only if a
   stronger single word replaces two weaker ones

## V. Application by Format

### Headlines

**Purpose:** Stop the reader. Earn the click or the continued read.

**Rules:**

- Front-load the key word. Readers scan from left to right and often
  read only the first three words.
- Use active verbs, not noun phrases. "Company Launches New Feature" is
  stronger than "New Feature Launch by Company."
- Specific numbers outperform vague quantifiers. "5 Ways to..." beats
  "Several Ways to..." Not because of SEO — because specificity signals
  that the writer did the work.
- Questions work when the reader genuinely wants the answer. "Is Your
  Password Secure?" works. "Have You Considered Our New Product?" does
  not.
- Avoid clickbait mechanics ("You Won't Believe...") — they erode trust
  even when they work short-term.

**Length:** 6-12 words. Under 6 may lack context. Over 12 loses the
scanning reader.

### Taglines

**Purpose:** Capture the essence of a brand, product, or campaign in a
memorable phrase.

**Rules:**

- A tagline is a promise, not a description. "Just Do It" promises
  empowerment. "Athletic Shoes for Sports" describes a product.
- It should work without the brand name. If the tagline only makes sense
  when paired with the logo, it is a caption, not a tagline.
- It should be speakable. If you wouldn't say it aloud to a friend, it
  is too stiff or too clever.
- Timelessness over trendiness. A tagline that references current slang
  will age poorly.

**Length:** 2-7 words. The best taglines are 3-5.

### Subject Lines

**Purpose:** Get the email opened.

**Rules:**

- Personalization works (name, company, specific reference) but only if
  genuine. Fake personalization ("Hey [FIRST_NAME]") is worse than none.
- Urgency works if honest. "Last day" means last day, not "we'll send
  this again tomorrow."
- Questions that the reader wants answered outperform statements.
- Preview text (the line after the subject) is part of the composition.
  Don't waste it on "View this email in your browser."

**Length:** 30-50 characters. Mobile truncation starts at ~35 characters.

### Button and CTA Text

**Purpose:** Tell the user exactly what will happen when they click.

**Rules:**

- Use first person when possible. "Start my free trial" outperforms
  "Start your free trial" in many contexts.
- Name the action, not the mechanism. "Get the report" is better than
  "Click here" or "Submit."
- Match the CTA to the commitment level. "Buy now" on a $10,000 product
  page is premature. "See pricing" is appropriate.

**Length:** 2-5 words.

### Product Names

**Purpose:** Be memorable, searchable, and suggestive of the product's
value.

**Rules:**

- Pronounceability matters. If someone cannot say the name aloud
  confidently, they will not recommend it to others.
- Avoid generic descriptors. "SmartSync Pro" sounds like every other
  SaaS product. A distinctive name creates a category rather than
  joining one.
- Check for unintended meanings, abbreviations, and sounds in other
  languages if the product is global.
- The name does not need to describe the product. "Apple," "Amazon,"
  and "Slack" describe nothing about their products. They are memorable,
  easy to say, and easy to spell.

## VI. Testing and Refinement

### The Five-Second Test

Show the headline, tagline, or name to someone for five seconds. Then
hide it. Ask: what do you remember? If they can repeat it or paraphrase
it, the text is memorable. If they cannot, it is forgettable.

### The Substitution Test

Can you replace your headline with a competitor's headline and have it
still make sense? If yes, the headline is too generic. It should be
specific enough that only your product, brand, or argument could use it.

### The Out-Loud Test

Read the text aloud. Does it sound natural? Does it have a rhythm? Do
any words stumble? If you would not say it in conversation, it probably
should not be a headline or tagline.

### The "So What?" Test

After reading the headline or tagline, ask: "So what?" If the text does
not immediately suggest why the reader should care, it needs revision.
The answer to "so what?" should be implicit in the text itself, not
requiring additional explanation.
